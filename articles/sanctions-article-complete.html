From Compliance to Strategy: How Sanctions Have Become a Systemic Risk Framework

Strategic Analysis by Daniel Beyene | Published January 2026

Introduction: The Sanctions Misdiagnosis

For most financial institutions, sanctions are still framed as a compliance question: Are we compliant or not?

That framing is no longer sufficient, and in some cases actively misleading.

Over the past decade, sanctions have evolved into a systemic risk mechanism that reshapes institutions far beyond screening and controls. They influence market access, correspondent banking relationships, governance structures, capital allocation, and even long-term business strategy. Yet many organizations continue to manage sanctions as a technical sub-function, delegated almost entirely to compliance teams and assessed through static risk tools.

This disconnect explains why sanctions failures often come as a surprise to senior management. Not because the rules were unclear, but because the nature of the risk was misunderstood.

Sanctions are no longer just a legal or compliance constraint. They function as a strategic risk framework, embedded in geopolitical power dynamics and enforced through the financial system itself. Institutions that treat sanctions narrowly expose themselves to blind spots that no amount of screening can compensate for.

Case in Point: The 'U-Turn' Mechanism

Prior to November 2008, U.S. sanctions regulations allowed a "U-Turn" exemption where non-U.S. banks could process dollar-denominated transactions for Iranian entities if they were initiated and ended offshore. The revocation of this license (31 CFR § 560.516) effectively weaponized the clearing infrastructure itself. It demonstrated that legal jurisdiction is not a matter of physical borders, but of the milliseconds a transaction spends "touching" a U.S. correspondent bank's ledger.

The Conceptual Error: Why Sanctions ≠ AML

A common source of confusion lies in the way sanctions are operationally grouped with AML and export controls. While this convergence may make sense from an efficiency standpoint, it often leads to conceptual errors with real consequences.

Anti-money laundering regimes are primarily designed to address financial flows derived from criminal activity. Their logic is probabilistic, transaction-focused, and backward-looking. Export controls, by contrast, regulate the movement of goods, technology, and know-how, often through licensing regimes and jurisdiction-specific rules.

Sanctions operate on a different plane altogether. As reflected in repeated policy statements by the US Department of the Treasury and European institutions, modern sanctions regimes are explicitly designed to influence behavior through financial and economic pressure, not merely to prohibit specific transactions. Their objective is not to detect crime, but to constrain behavior of states, entities, sectors, or individuals by restricting access to financial and economic systems. Enforcement is not merely punitive; it is strategic, designed to alter incentives and reshape conduct over time.

The problem arises when institutions attempt to force sanctions risk into AML logic:

  • Screening replaces judgment
  • Lists replace context
  • Technical controls replace strategic analysis

Operational convergence has created the illusion of conceptual equivalence. Treating sanctions as an extension of AML weakens both frameworks. AML tools are asked to solve problems they were never designed for, while sanctions risk is reduced to a binary exercise detached from its geopolitical and strategic foundations.

Most sanctions failures do not stem from poor implementation. They stem from category errors—from misunderstanding what sanctions are meant to do, and how they exert pressure through the financial system.

Case in Point: The 2022 Oligarch Designations

The mass designation of Russian business elites following the invasion of Ukraine exposed the limits of AML-centric risk modeling. Many of these individuals had passed decades of rigorous AML and "Source of Wealth" checks. They were not "criminals" in the traditional sense; they were strategic targets. Institutions relying on criminal-probability models (AML) were unprepared for a risk shift defined by geopolitical association rather than transaction illegality.

To fully grasp this dynamic, one must confront a feature of modern sanctions regimes that institutions often describe as unfair or excessive, but which is in fact central to their effectiveness: extraterritoriality.

Extraterritoriality Is Not a Bug, It Is the Design

Few features of modern sanctions regimes generate as much discomfort within financial institutions as extraterritorial enforcement. It is often perceived as excessive, unpredictable, or incompatible with principles of legal certainty. Yet this perception obscures a critical point: extraterritoriality is not an unintended consequence of sanctions policy. It is a central element of its effectiveness.

Contemporary sanctions regimes, particularly those administered by the United States derive much of their force from their interaction with the global financial system. Access to key currencies, clearing mechanisms, and correspondent banking networks creates points of leverage that extend well beyond national borders. In this context, jurisdiction is not defined solely by physical presence, but by financial connectivity. This reliance on financial connectivity as a point of leverage is openly acknowledged in US sanctions policy and enforcement guidance, where access to clearing, correspondent banking, and the dollar system is treated as a central transmission mechanism.

For global financial institutions, this reality challenges traditional assumptions about "local compliance." A transaction that is lawful under domestic law may still create exposure when it intersects with foreign sanctions regimes through currency use, counterparties, or financial infrastructure. The relevant question is therefore not where an institution is incorporated, but how it participates in the international financial system.

This dynamic explains why sanctions risk cannot be effectively managed through a purely jurisdictional lens. It also highlights the limits of relying on formal legal boundaries to assess exposure. Extraterritorial enforcement reflects the strategic objective of sanctions: to project economic influence through financial interdependence, rather than territorial control.

Figure 2: How U.S. sanctions jurisdiction extends beyond borders through financial infrastructure and persons. The same transaction faces different enforcement exposure depending on USD involvement and U.S. person participation.

From an institutional perspective, the issue is not whether extraterritoriality is legitimate or desirable. It is a structural condition that must be understood and integrated into risk assessment and decision-making. Failing to do so results in reactive compliance responses, often triggered only after exposure has materialized.

Recognizing extraterritoriality as a design feature rather than a policy anomaly allows institutions to move beyond defensive postures. It reframes sanctions risk as an inherent aspect of operating within a globally interconnected financial system, one that requires anticipation, contextual analysis, and informed judgment rather than reliance on formal compliance boundaries alone.

Understanding extraterritoriality in these terms brings into focus a second, often overlooked dimension of sanctions risk: the indirect and cumulative effects that extend well beyond enforcement actions themselves.

The Hidden Second-Order Effects Institutions Often Underestimate

Sanctions risk is frequently assessed through the lens of direct exposure: prohibited transactions, designated counterparties, and the potential for enforcement action. While these elements are essential, they capture only a portion of the broader impact sanctions regimes have on financial institutions.

More consequential, in many cases, are the second-order effects that emerge over time. These effects are less visible, more difficult to quantify, and therefore often underrepresented in formal risk assessments.

Figure 3: Most institutions have robust controls for direct effects (Layer 1) but lack frameworks to anticipate operational and strategic impacts (Layers 2-3), where the greatest institutional damage occurs.

One such effect is the gradual reshaping of correspondent banking relationships. As sanctions regimes expand and enforcement expectations tighten, institutions may reassess the viability of certain corridors, jurisdictions, or client segments. These decisions are rarely driven by a single regulatory event. Instead, they reflect an accumulation of perceived risk, uncertainty, and operational burden. The resulting contraction of correspondent networks can have long-term implications for market access and service continuity, particularly in higher-risk regions.

Client strategy is another area where indirect effects are felt. Sanctions considerations increasingly influence onboarding decisions, sectoral exposure, and geographic focus. Over time, this can lead to selection bias, where institutions concentrate on lower-risk profiles at the expense of diversification and growth. While such shifts may reduce short-term compliance pressure, they can also constrain strategic flexibility and revenue potential.

Sanctions regimes also generate reputational effects that extend beyond formal breaches. Association with sanctioned jurisdictions or entities, whether direct or indirect, can alter how institutions are perceived by regulators, counterparties, and investors. These perceptions, once formed, are difficult to reverse and may affect supervisory intensity, funding costs, or partnership opportunities.

Importantly, these second-order effects tend to interact. De-risking decisions influence correspondent relationships, which in turn shape client offerings and market presence. Over time, sanctions risk becomes embedded not only in control functions, but in the institution's operating model itself. International institutions such as the BIS, IMF, and World Bank have indeed, repeatedly highlighted how sanctions pressure and compliance expectations contribute to de-risking dynamics and the contraction of correspondent banking relationships.

Because these dynamics unfold gradually, they are often addressed reactively rather than strategically. Traditional risk frameworks, focused on discrete events and binary outcomes, struggle to capture cumulative exposure. As a result, institutions may underestimate the extent to which sanctions regimes are already influencing their business models even in the absence of enforcement action.

Figure 4: The cascading impact of sanctions events. Direct effects are immediate and visible; operational effects emerge over weeks to months; strategic impacts compound over months to years. Traditional risk assessments focus on the center while strategic frameworks account for the entire system.

Recognizing these indirect effects does not imply that they can or should be eliminated. Rather, it underscores the need for a broader perspective on sanctions risk, one that accounts for how regulatory pressure reshapes institutional behavior over time.

If sanctions regimes affect not only transactions but also strategy and structure, the question then becomes where responsibility for these trade-offs truly lies, within control functions, or at the level of governance and senior decision-making.

Why Traditional Risk Assessments Fail to Capture Sanctions Reality

Most sanctions risk assessments are built around identifiable events: designated entities, prohibited transactions, and jurisdictional exposure. These elements are necessary, but they reflect an assumption that sanctions risk is largely static and observable. In practice, sanctions regimes are dynamic, and their impact often materializes through uncertainty rather than clear prohibition.

One limitation of traditional risk assessments is their reliance on periodic reviews. Sanctions environments, however, can evolve rapidly in response to geopolitical developments. New designations, interpretative guidance, or enforcement priorities may significantly alter exposure within short timeframes. Static assessments struggle to account for this fluidity, particularly when they are anchored to historical data or fixed risk indicators.

Another constraint lies in the emphasis on formal compliance boundaries. Risk is often evaluated based on legal permissibility at a given point in time, rather than on forward-looking considerations. This approach may underestimate exposure arising from transactions or relationships that are technically permissible but strategically sensitive. As a result, institutions may find themselves compliant in form yet exposed in substance.

Traditional frameworks also tend to privilege quantifiable metrics. While useful for reporting and comparison, such metrics can obscure qualitative factors that are central to sanctions risk, including geopolitical context, enforcement posture, and reputational sensitivity. These elements are difficult to score, yet they often determine how sanctions risk is perceived by authorities and counterparties.

Finally, many assessments assume a clear separation between sanctions risk and other risk categories. However, sanctions interact with credit risk, operational risk, reputational risk, and strategic risk. Treating sanctions in isolation can therefore lead to incomplete conclusions and misplaced confidence in mitigation measures.

These limitations do not suggest that risk assessments are ineffective. Rather, they highlight the need to complement them with judgment, contextual analysis, and mechanisms that allow institutions to adapt to evolving sanctions landscapes. This limitation echoes broader governance observations found in FATF and Basel Committee materials, which emphasize that risk management frameworks must be complemented by judgment and senior-level accountability. Without such adaptation, assessments risk becoming exercises in documentation rather than tools for informed decision-making.

Recognizing these limitations raises a final question: how should institutions organize themselves to manage sanctions risk in a way that reflects its strategic, dynamic, and interconnected nature?

Towards a Strategic Sanctions Framework

Moving beyond a purely compliance-oriented approach does not require institutions to abandon existing controls or frameworks. Rather, it requires placing them within a broader structure that acknowledges the strategic nature of sanctions risk and the role of senior decision-making.

A strategic sanctions framework begins with clear ownership. Institutions benefit from explicitly defining where responsibility for sanctions-related trade-offs resides, particularly when decisions affect market presence, client strategy, or long-term exposure. While compliance functions provide essential expertise, strategic accountability cannot be fully delegated.

Integration with business strategy is equally important. Sanctions considerations should be embedded into decisions on market entry, product development, and correspondent relationships, rather than addressed retrospectively. This integration allows institutions to anticipate constraints and align their risk appetite with evolving geopolitical realities.

Forward-looking analysis forms another core element. Scenario-based thinking, examining how potential changes in sanctions regimes might affect existing exposures can help institutions move from reactive adjustment to informed preparation. Such analysis does not aim to predict outcomes, but to improve institutional readiness for a range of plausible developments.

Case in Point: Maritime Asymmetry

The emergence of the "Shadow Fleet"—hundreds of aging tankers with opaque ownership and deactivated transponders—represents a systemic challenge to the G7 price cap regime. This isn't a list-matching problem; it's a structural maritime security risk. A strategic framework must look beyond the name on the bill of lading to the physical and insurance-based "transmission mechanisms" of the trade itself.

Effective escalation mechanisms are also essential. Institutions should ensure that sanctions-related concerns are communicated in a manner that supports decision-making at senior levels, without over-reliance on technical detail or binary indicators. The objective is not to simplify complexity, but to present it in a form that enables informed judgment.

Finally, a strategic framework recognizes the limits of control. Sanctions risk cannot be fully eliminated and attempts to do so may result in excessive de-risking or strategic paralysis. Instead, institutions must make conscious choices about how much exposure they are willing to accept and under what conditions.

Conclusion — Understanding, Not Eliminating, Sanctions Risk

Sanctions regimes have become a defining feature of the global financial landscape. Their influence extends well beyond compliance functions, shaping institutional behavior, strategic choices, and governance structures. Treating sanctions as a technical obligation risks overlooking these broader effects.

Effective management of sanctions risk requires acknowledging its systemic nature. This involves understanding how sanctions operate as instruments of economic policy, how they interact with financial infrastructure, and how they influence institutional decision-making over time.

For financial institutions, the challenge is not to achieve perfect insulation from sanctions exposure, but to develop the capacity to understand, anticipate, and own the risks involved. Doing so demands judgment, clarity of responsibility, and a willingness to engage with sanctions as a strategic reality rather than a peripheral compliance concern.

References & Further Reading

1. Core Regulatory & Policy Sources

US Department of the Treasury – 2024/2025 National Proliferation Financing Risk Assessment Provides the definitive map of how the US government views the intersection of national security and the global financial system, essential for identifying the "behavioral" risks.
OFAC – Economic Sanctions Enforcement Guidelines (31 CFR Part 501) Outlines the qualitative criteria, such as management awareness and institutional "willfulness" that determine the severity of enforcement, proving that technical compliance is secondary to institutional intent.
European Commission – Basic Principles on the Use of Restrictive Measures Articulates the philosophical framework of EU "smart" sanctions, highlighting the move away from broad embargoes toward targeted economic pressure designed to alter political incentives.

2. Governance, Risk & Financial System Sources

Basel Committee (BCBS) – Corporate Governance Principles for Banks Establishes the global standard for Board-level accountability, reinforcing that sanctions risk is a non-delegable strategic responsibility rather than a back-office compliance task.
Financial Action Task Force (FATF) – Guidance on the Risk-Based Approach The primary global authority on moving beyond "check-the-box" compliance, providing the theoretical grounding for the "judgment and context" required to manage modern risk.
Bank for International Settlements (BIS) – Reports on Correspondent Banking Offers a technical yet systemic view of how regulatory pressure "erodes" the physical infrastructure of global payments, leading to the financial fragmentation.

3. Systemic & Second-Order Effects

International Monetary Fund (IMF) – Global Financial Stability Reports (GFSR) Analyzes how geopolitical tensions translate into systemic financial instability, providing the macro-economic data that links individual sanctions to global "de-risking" trends.
World Bank – Publications on Financial Exclusion and De-risking Quantifies the real-world economic cost of excessive de-risking, offering a critical perspective on the "second-order" damage caused when institutions prioritize isolation over strategic risk ownership
